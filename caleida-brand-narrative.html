What we believe,
and why Caleida exists. The story we tell ourselves before we tell anyone else. Read it when you join. Re-read it when a decision feels hard — the answer is almost always in here.

The belief

In every home there is a person who remembers. Who notices the milk is low and the dentist is overdue and the school jumper no longer fits. None of it is written down. All of it is carried.

We call that weight the mental load — and for most of history it has been invisible, unpaid, and unevenly shared. Not because anyone is cruel. Because no one was noticing.

Caleida exists to make the invisible visible — and then to help carry it. Not by nagging. Not by shaming. By standing beside the person who could be doing more, and quietly making it easier to step up.

The problem we refuse to accept

The work that no one sees is the work that breaks people

The dishes get done and the appointments get made — but the remembering, the planning, the worrying, the holding-it-all-in-your-head — that almost always lands on one person. It is real labour, and it has a cost.

01
It's invisible. You can't split a load no one can see. The first move isn't fairness — it's making the weight legible to the whole household.
02
It's lopsided. One person becomes the household's operating system. The other waits to be told what to do — and being told is itself a tax on the person who carries the list.
03
It's corrosive. Resentment doesn't arrive in arguments. It accumulates in the thousand small things one partner tracked and the other never knew about.
04
Apps made it worse. Shared calendars and to-do lists just moved the load. Someone still has to fill them in, assign them, and chase them. We didn't need another list. We needed a mate.
The name

Caleida, and the one we call Cal

From caleidoscopio — kaleidoscope. Hundreds of small, scattered fragments that, turned the right way and held to the light, resolve into one clear picture. That is exactly what a household is, and exactly what we do with it.

Caleida
The platform. The company. The pattern. All the fragments of a family's life — the shop, the dentist, the half-term, the kit that doesn't fit — gathered and turned until they make sense. The kaleidoscope is the promise: chaos into clarity.
Cal
The mate you actually talk to. Caleida is the system; Cal is the human face of it. Short, warm, first-name. You don't open Cal. You text him — and he texts back like someone who's known your house for years.

The name carries our two markets in one word. It reads as English and lives in Spanish — fitting for a product born between the UK and Spain, for families who don't all share one language but share the same load.

Who we serve

We talk to the dad. We're building for the whole house.

This is the most important strategic line in the document, so we say it plainly: the person we speak to is not always the person we're for. Getting this relationship right is the whole game.

The user we talk to
The dad who wants to step up
He isn't lazy and he isn't the villain. He's a partner who genuinely wants to carry more but doesn't have the map in his head — so he waits to be asked, and asking is exhausting for the person asking. Cal hands him the map.
The person we're for
The partner who's been carrying it
She is the ultimate beneficiary — but she is never the one being managed. Success isn't her getting better reminders. It's her stopping being the reminder. The relief we're really chasing is hers.

So we account for one and serve the other. Cal lives in the dad's pocket, but every feature is judged by a question asked from the partner's side of the bed: does this take weight off me, or just move it around?

The product, in pictures

How Caleida works — a story in six panels

The idea, the interface, and the everyday use of it — told the way it actually happens in a house. The entire surface area is two things: a chat thread with Cal, and one screen called The Load. Everything else is voice and timing.

That's the entire experience, by design. No project-management cosplay, no shared backlog to groom. A mate who texts at the right moment, an interface that's just a reply, and one honest picture of the load. If a feature can't live inside one of those six panels, it probably shouldn't exist.

What we believe

Five principles. They settle arguments.

These aren't decoration. When a feature, a notification, or a line of copy is in question, it has to pass these. If it can't, it doesn't ship — no matter how clever it is.

01
Warm roast, never shame
Cal teases like a good friend, not a disappointed parent. Tone is a product requirement, not a finish. A message that could make a dad defensive or deflated is a bug — we'd rather say nothing than make someone feel small.
02
Make the invisible visible
You can't share what you can't see. Our first job is always to surface the load honestly — the real picture of who carries what — before we try to redistribute a single thing. Clarity before fairness.
03
Noticing, not outsourcing
We are not a butler that takes the household off the dad's plate. We're the nudge that helps him notice and own it himself. The win isn't a task completed by Cal — it's a partner who started seeing the load on his own. Growth, not dependence.
04
Earn the right to speak
Cal acts unprompted — but proactivity is a privilege, not a license. Every nudge must be timely, useful, and worth the interruption. If we wouldn't text a friend about it, we don't ping a user about it.
05
Privacy is a promise, not a setting
We hold the most tender data a family has — children's ages, health, the honest truth of how a home really runs. We treat that as sacred: minimal, encrypted, never sold, never trained on. Trust is the product. Lose it once and there is no second household.
How Cal sounds

The voice is the moat

Anyone can build a reminder engine. No one else has Cal. The voice is what makes a notification feel like a mate checking in instead of an app demanding attention — and it's the one thing we guard hardest.

Cal is
  • A mate who's got your back
  • Dry, warm, a little cheeky
  • On your side, always
  • Brief — he texts, he doesn't lecture
  • "9 days since the big shop, mate. Fridge is looking brave."
Cal is never
  • A nagging parent or a boss
  • Shaming, guilt-tripping, passive-aggressive
  • A productivity drill sergeant
  • Corporate, clinical, or fake-cheerful
  • "You have 4 overdue tasks. Complete them now."

Test for every line Cal says: would a good friend — one who genuinely likes you — actually say this? If it sounds like software, rewrite it. If it could land as a dig, kill it.

Why it matters

We're not organising chores. We're rebalancing homes.

It would be easy to describe Caleida as a household task app. That description is true and completely misses the point.

The mental load is one of the quiet structural inequalities of modern family life. It shapes who gets to rest, who gets to work, whose career bends, and how much resentment pools under the surface of an otherwise good relationship. It is enormous, and it is almost entirely undiscussed.

If we do our job, a partner somewhere exhales — because for the first time, she isn't the only one holding it all.

That's the company. Not features. Not funnels. A measurable amount of weight lifted off the people who've been carrying their households alone — and a generation of partners who learned to notice. Everything we build is in service of that exhale.

In one breath

Caleida turns the scattered fragments of family life into one clear picture — and gives it a voice, Cal, a mate who notices what's slipping and nudges the partner who could help to step up. Warm roast, never shame. Noticing, not outsourcing. We talk to the dad; we're building for the relief of the person who's been carrying it all.
