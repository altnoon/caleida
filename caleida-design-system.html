01

Logo & wordmark

One mark, one idea. The kaleidoscope simplified to its four facets — the shares of a household resolving into a single picture. Cream gaps turn the segments into facets; the 45° turn reads as a gem, not a pie chart. It's the whole logo, and the same mark carries from a 74px lockup to a 16px favicon. No terracotta "i", no second symbol — the name is Caleida, the mate is Cal, the mark is the mark. The wordmark is set in Bricolage Grotesque — a warm, modern grotesque that stays quiet and lets the diamond carry the craft.

Primary lockup — horizontal
caleida

Mark + wordmark. The workhorse — nav bars, footers, decks, letterhead. Colour lives in the mark; the wordmark is quiet ink.

The mark up close — and the stacked lockup
caleida

Four facets, cream gaps, turned to a diamond. Stacked lockup for the app splash and avatars. (Colour order of the quadrants is adjustable — keep terracotta where the eye lands first.)

Wordmark alone
caleida

Where the mark can't sit — body text, legal, tight lockups. Plain ink; the mark carries the colour elsewhere.

App icon / avatar

The mark on warm paper stock — handmade among glass apps.

Favicon — the same mark

One flag at every size. Below ~20px the gaps close and the diamond does the work — no separate small-size mark.

Clear space & minimum size
caleida

Keep clear space equal to the mark's width on all sides. Minimum wordmark width 96px on screen; below that, use the mark alone.

Never
✕Recolour the mark or the wordmark outside the palette
✕Add gloss, bevel, long shadow, or outline
✕Stretch, condense, or re-space the letters
✕Lift the kaleidoscope out of the logo to use as a motif
✕Set the wordmark in any face other than Bricolage Grotesque

Colour has a job
Terracotta — the brand, calls to action, the number that matters. Used with intent, never as wallpaper.
Teal — Cal acting on your behalf: a confirm, a logged win, a task taken off her plate.
Gold — small warmth: eyebrows, labels, a highlight. Never for large areas or text.
Ink on paper — the default. If a screen needs a third colour to work, the layout is the problem.
Evening / dark surface (product night mode)

Caleida is often opened after the house is quiet. The dark surface is espresso #211913, not black — paper's night twin. Terracotta warms to #D77E57 for legibility on dark, teal to #7FB6A6. Same warmth, lights down.

03

Typography

Four faces, each with one job. The logotype is a warm modern grotesque; headlines think in a serif; the interface reads in a clean sans; and one voice is all Cal's — his handwriting. Cal doesn't type at you, he leaves you a note, so his words are set in a warm hand while the interface around them stays clean.

Aa
Bricolage Grotesque

The logotype — the wordmark, and only the wordmark. A warm grotesque with hand-drawn quirks; modern and quiet, so the diamond carries the craft. Weight 600, tracking −0.02em.

Aa
Fraunces

Editorial display and headlines only. Weights 440–560 — lightened from 620, so it reads elegant rather than chunky. The literate warmth of the brand.

Aa
Plus Jakarta Sans

Body, UI, labels, buttons — the whole interface. Weights 400–800. Legible, unfluffy, does the reading.

Aa
Shantell Sans

Cal's voice. His handwriting, on the notes he leaves — chat bubbles and taped notes. Weight 500. Cal writes; he doesn't type.

04

Material & surface

Where the editorial mind meets the kitchen. Surfaces are real: warm paper stock, honest shadows from one light source, a press into the paper on the big moments, and a strip of tape where something deserves to be kept. Tactility is felt, never loud.

Paper stock

A faint warm gradient plus a whisper of grain. The page breathes; it never looks like flat #fff.

Shadow — one light source, top-left

Three elevations, all warm-tinted (never grey), all agreeing on the same light. Rest / raised / lifted.

Letterpress & tape
caleida

Display type presses into the paper (a 1px light-line). Tape is punctuation — one taped element per screen, on the thing worth keeping.

MATERIAL — ALWAYS
  • Texture lives in surfaces; text sits flat and crisp on top
  • Every shadow obeys the top-left light
  • Letterpress only on display type and the logo
  • One taped element per screen, maximum
MATERIAL — NEVER
  • Leather, stitching, wood veneer — iOS-2010 skeuomorphism stays dead
  • Grain or texture behind body text
  • Gloss, bevel, or glow on anything
  • Cold grey shadows, or shadows from two directions
05

Imagery

Two streams, one rule: watercolour for people, photography for things. The illustrated world tells the brand's stories; photographed objects furnish the product. They never blur — a scene is drawn, an object is shot.

Stream 1 — watercolour illustration · for people & stories

The existing storyboard world — soft ink line, warm wash, domestic truth. Home for the landing page, the narrative, campaigns, the six-panel story. This is where Caleida shows people, and it stays hand-drawn.

Stream 2 — photographed objects · for the product
bins tonight
WK 29

Real household objects on real surfaces — notes, labels, a glazed mug, the week-sheet — shot with the same top-left light. Home for product screens, feature cards, app store. Props above are stand-ins; final art is photographed to this spec.

The line between them

If it has a face, it's watercolour. If it's a thing on a surface, it's photography. Neither ever quotes the kaleidoscope — the mark stays in the logo. Both share the palette, the warmth, and the one light source, so a drawn scene and a shot object feel like the same home.

The household’s work, made visible

The system earns its name on one screen: Invisible Work. This is the vocabulary that keeps “showing the work” from curdling into the chore-tracker the brand refuses to be — whose the work is, where it stands, and what’s coming, held as a mirror rather than a scoreboard.

The line we hold: showing tasks and "remaining" is one bad decision away from the task app the brand rejects. So: no red, no "overdue," no urgency-shaming; the partner's work is shown with equal warmth (Cal never tells tales); and Cal offers to take things, never assigns them. Visibility, not accountability theatre.

Who carries it — the person colours

One colour per person, always paired with an initial so it never relies on colour alone. The two humans are the brand’s two pigments; Cal is the whole-house kaleidoscope.

The framing rules — how this stays a companion

The visual vocabulary is only half of it. These rules keep "showing the work" from curdling into the task app the brand refuses to be.

ALWAYS
  • Colour = who carries it, always with an initial. One system across every screen.
  • "Still open," never "overdue." Open loops are neutral, not failures.
  • Her work shown with equal warmth. Visibility, not surveillance.
  • Cal offers to take, you choose. He brokers; nobody gets assigned.
  • Handled fades. Done work recedes toward calm, not a trophy wall.
NEVER
  • No red, no alarms, no urgency badges. Warmth can't shame.
  • No shared editable backlog. Cal is the broker, not a board you both poke.
  • No streaks, points, or completion %. The mirror stays a mirror.
  • No "she didn't do X" framing. Never a faction between partners.
  • No infinite lists. Not this week's loop? Cal holds it off-screen.

09

Motion & behaviour

Cal moves the way he talks: rarely, and with good timing. Motion confirms, it never performs.

Pace

Transitions 160–220ms, ease-out like a drawer closing softly. Nothing bounces for attention.

The press

Primary buttons depress 1px on tap — a physical acknowledgement, not a ripple. Paper responds to touch.

Silence is a feature

No celebration animations, no confetti, no badge-shine. The exhale is calm — the interface goes quiet, it doesn't cheer.
